Muʻo in San Francisco

our hosts

This trip does not happen without you. The campus access, the scheduled time, the honest conversations, and the small moments our muʻo will be talking about at each reunion — you gave them a great glimpse into this industry, and a real reason to believe there is a place for them in it.

Day 1

Apple

Cupertino, California

with Eddie Fan

Eddie welcomed us to Apple's Cupertino campus, and the intentionality was immediate — in the architecture and design of the company that pioneered UX/UI. Our muʻo reflected on Eddie's "becoming journey" and appreciated his practical breakdown of the skills and demands tied to each tech role. The candid conversation about AI shaping the workforce — the disruption, the opportunity, and what actually matters for the next generation — was exactly what they needed to hear. Learning about Eddie's proximity to features used daily on the iPhone, and his recollection of meetings with Tim Cook, was pretty epic as well.

To close, Eddie sent us off with turmeric energy shots — a welcomed boost heading into the rest of the week.

After hours

We celebrated Mokihana's 21st birthday at the iconic Original Joe's, in the birthday section that went off every five minutes. The butter cake, if you haven't had it, is something else. We got it twice over the span of the trip, which is saying something.

Day 2 AM

Salesforce

San Francisco, California

with Arlene Tanaka

Upon entry we unknowingly stepped into an amusement park ride, shooting 1,070 feet above the city into the ʻOhana Floor, where 360-degree views of the Bay Area stunned our virgin eyes. The energy up there was unlike anything we experienced at any other company — busy, young, and alive. And then there was the rooftop garden. ʻĀina mid-city, yes please.

Mahalo Arlene for a visit that had a little bit of everything.

Day 2 PM

Elemental
Interactive

San Francisco, California

with Tiffany Huynh

After Salesforce, we headed to Elemental Interactive — a startup pitching environment unlike anything else on the trip. We watched live pitches, asked hard questions, and got an inside look at what early-stage startup culture actually looks and feels like.

The energy was raw, creative, and a little chaotic in the best way — exactly what you'd hope a startup to be. Our muʻo left with a clearer picture of what building something from scratch looks like, and a healthy respect for anyone who tries.

After hours

We ended the night at Pacific Cocktail Haven (trust me, get the Thrilla in Manila) — a decided must — and walked a few blocks uphill to The Public Izakaya for the best karaage chicken and worst ramen of our lives, mainly because Victor's ramen had potato chips on top.

Day 3

Lucasfilm

Letterman Digital Arts Center · San Francisco, California

with Jay Kashiwabara, Kim Holden, Nakoa Choy, Wes Culver

Third year visiting the Lucasfilm campus and it does not get old. The wall art and foreign film posters alone deserve their own walking tour, never mind the memorabilia lining every hallway. Paradise.

The campus tour, the film screening, and the conversation that followed all pointed in the same direction: perseverance — and apparently some light stalker tendencies. One muʻo said, "I think I laughed more during this visit than in my entire three years living up here." Another noted how refreshing it was to find that the people working at such a high level are genuinely down to earth and invested in offering really practical advice.

After hours

We made our way to Chinatown to soak up a bit of culture, but most things were closing, so we tracked down egg tarts and ate them on the sidewalk surrounded by street art and trash cans piled high. It felt authentic, a little like home, but tasted a bit better.

Day 4

Google

San Francisco, California

with Lyn Meheʻula Spiesman, Brandon Cavins, Shawn Carter, Makoa Santarini

Lyn opened with news that a muʻo from last year's trip was starting at Google, and just like that, eyes were wide and possibilities palpable. The group's unofficial slogan for the rest of the day was pretty much "open to work… only for Google." Which feels on trend considering the Kamehameha Schools alumni already there — thriving and holding it down at one of the most recognized companies in the world. Their input on adaptability was top notch, and so were their stories of hustle in young startups. One muʻo mentioned Google was their highlight because of the one-on-one conversations and how easy it was to relate to the people in the room.

But let's not forget the most important moment of the day: Jaden ordering the "hah-leh kah-leh" salad.

Mahalo everyone at Google for the warmth, the aloha, and for showing our muʻo what the path can look like when someone like them walks it well.

Day 5

Meta

Menlo Park, California

with Jeremiah Chung, Jon Lee

Welcome to Disneyland. Just kidding — but the Meta campus was designed by Frank Gehry, the same architect that designed Disney Village, and the vibes were committed. Jeremiah and Jon welcomed us warmly and had the utmost patience walking us through a massive campus in our very special "business colored" badges. We loved the retro Japanese arcade machines, the music room, and the free froyo.

Walking through V1 of the new buildings we stopped at a poster that read "building the future of connection." Expecting an answer that involved a virtual metaverse, I asked what Jeremiah thought the future of connection looked like. He said something along the lines of exactly what we were doing right then — being in person, together, building community. It landed. Especially in Meta.

I hope you catch a glimpse of those foxes, guys.

After hours

Last night in the Bay. The muʻo closed out the week the way they started it — together.

"I think I laughed more during this visit than in my entire three years living up here."

— a muʻo, after Lucasfilm
